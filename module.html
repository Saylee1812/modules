Module 1: Anti-Doping Awareness

1. Prohibited Substances and Methods

World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) Prohibited List: Athletes must be familiar with the WADA Prohibited List, which is updated annually. It includes substances and methods that are banned both in and out of competition.

Categories of Prohibited Substances:

  • Anabolic Agents: Steroids that increase muscle mass and strength.
  • Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, and Related Substances: Such as EPO (Erythropoietin), which enhances oxygen delivery to muscles.
  • Beta-2 Agonists: Used to treat asthma but can be performance-enhancing in high doses.
  • Hormone and Metabolic Modulators: Substances that influence the hormonal balance, like anti-estrogens.
  • Diuretics and Masking Agents: Used to manipulate body weight and hide the presence of other prohibited substances.

Prohibited Methods:

  • Blood Doping: The use of transfusions or EPO to increase red blood cell count.
  • Gene Doping: Altering genes to enhance athletic performance.

2. Athlete Responsibilities

Strict Liability Principle: Athletes are strictly liable for any prohibited substance found in their body, regardless of intent.

Therapeutic Use Exemptions (TUEs): Athletes with medical conditions requiring the use of a prohibited substance must apply for a TUE, which allows them to use the medication legally.

Knowledge of Rules: Athletes must stay informed about the anti-doping rules and regulations relevant to their sport and regularly review updates to the WADA Prohibited List.

3. Doping Control Process

Testing: Athletes can be tested at any time, both in-competition and out-of-competition. Testing can include urine, blood, or other biological samples.

Whereabouts Information: Elite athletes in a registered testing pool must provide their whereabouts information to allow for no-notice testing. Failure to provide accurate whereabouts information can lead to violations.

Sample Collection: The doping control officer collects samples following strict procedures to ensure the integrity and confidentiality of the sample.

B-Sample: If the initial A-sample tests positive for a prohibited substance, the athlete has the right to request analysis of the B-sample for confirmation.

4. Sanctions and Consequences

Sanctions for Violations: Penalties for doping violations can include disqualification of results, suspension from competition, and permanent bans in severe cases.

Impact on Team Results: If a team sport athlete is found guilty of a doping violation, it can result in sanctions for the entire team, including disqualification of results.

Rehabilitation and Education: Athletes who commit violations may be required to undergo anti-doping education and rehabilitation programs before they can return to competition.

5. Anti-Doping Organizations (ADOs)

Roles and Responsibilities: Organizations like WADA, national anti-doping agencies (e.g., USADA, UKAD), and International Federations (IFs) are responsible for enforcing anti-doping rules, conducting testing, and educating athletes.

Reporting and Whistleblowing: Athletes and support personnel are encouraged to report any knowledge or suspicions of doping practices through anonymous whistleblowing programs.

6. Fair Play and Integrity

Promoting Clean Sport: Athletes are expected to compete with integrity, respecting the rules of fair play.

Education: Continuous education on anti-doping is critical. Athletes, coaches, and support personnel should participate in regular anti-doping workshops and courses.
